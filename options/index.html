Stock Options Trading

History

Who traded the first option?

In 350 B.C. the philospher Thales anticiapted an abundant olive harvest in the coming year

In order to profit from this he put down deposits to tie up all of the local olive presses

When the prediction came true Thales profited handsomely by renting out the presses at an inflated rate

For a small amount of up front capital, Thales correct prediction netted him large gains.

What would have happened if Thales was wrong?

He would only have lost his initial deposit

Today we would say that Thales purchased a Call Option on olives

Definitions

What the heck is an option?

An option a type of Derivitive contract based on an underlying asset

In theory this can be anything from commodities, currencies, real estate, equities, future events, etc.

Like currencies and stock trading, there are two sides of the transaction, a buyer (Owner) and a seller (Writer)

Today option trading has become standardized and in North America are traded on the Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE)

The CBOE was created in 1973 and initiated the first modern options market with guaranteed settlement, standardized prices, expiration and contract size

An option Owner has the Right, but not the obligation to Exercise the option contract at a stipulated price (Strike Price) for a specified period of time (Expiration Date)

An option Writer has the Obligation to honour the option contract at the strike price if the option owner chooses to exercise it. Once the option expires or it is exercised the writer has no further obligations

Types of Option Contracts

What are Calls and Puts?

Call Option

A Call is a contract that grants its owner the right, but not the obligation, to Buy 100 shares of a specific underlying stock at a Fixed Strike Price per share, on or before a specific Expiration Date

The owner purchases this contract from the writer for a Premium

The owner has three courses of action that they can take:


  • Allow the option to expire worthless, which generally occurs when the current market value of the underlying asset remains below the strike price

  • Sell the contract to someone else on the exchange, which could result in a profit or loss depending on if the owner can sell the option at a higher price than the original premium paid

  • Exercise the option and buy 100 shares at the strike price which will require sufficient capital at time of exercise

It is called a Call Option because if exercised, the option writer will have the underlying stock "called" away from them

Call Example

It is Jan 1 and Google is trading at $500

Jill has $1500

Jill could purchase 3 shares of Google for $1500 and own 3 shares

Alternatively, Jill could buy an April/550 call option for a premium of $15.00 a share paying $1500 to control 100 shares of Google

Assume Google rises to $580

If Jill owned the stock she could sell and realize an $80 profit per share, or $240

Alternatively, if Jill owned the call option she can sell her call option for $30 per share, gaining $3000 in revenue. Minus her initial outlay she makes $1500

This strategy is called buying an Out of the Money long call option.

It is a highly speculative strategy that allows you to leverage small capital into large gains, providing that the underlying stock rises enough before expiration

Put Option

A Put is a contract that grants its owner the right, but not the obligation, to Sell 100 shares of a specific underlying stock at a Fixed Strike Price per share, on or before a specific Expiration Date

The owner purchases this contract from the writer for a Premium

The owner has three courses of action that they can take:


  • Allow the option to expire worthless, which generally occurs when the current market value of the underlying asset remains above the strike price

  • Sell the contract to someone else on the exchange, which could result in a profit or loss depending on if the owner can sell the option at a higher price than the original premium paid

  • Exercise the option and sell 100 shares at the strike price which will require 100 shares of the stock at time of exercise

It is called a Put Option because if exercised, the option writer will have the underlying stock "put" to them

Put Example

It is Jan 1 and GOOG is trading at $500

Jill owns 100 shares of GOOG and is concerned about GOOG dropping but still wants to profit if it rises

Jill buys an April/500 put option for a premium of $5.00 a share paying $500 to protect 100 shares of GOOG

Assume GOOG drops to $450

If Jill didn't buy the put option she would have lost $50 per share or -$5000 total.

If Jill owned the put option she can sell her put option for $50 per share, netting a $4500 gain to offset her stock losses which would now be -$500

This strategy is called buying an At The Money protective put option. It allows you to protect an existing position against a drop in value for a relatively small premium, similiar to insurance.

Option Moneyness

Huh? That's a word?

"In finance, moneyness is the relative position of the current price (or future price) of an underlying asset (e.g., a stock) with respect to the strike price of a derivative" (Wikipedia)

Out of the money options are options whose strike price is such that the option has no intrinsic value at current market prices

Examples of Out of the Money Options

  • A 550 call when the current stock price is less than 550
  • A 20 put when the current stock price is greater than 20
  • In selling the option you will lose ~ the difference

At the money options are options whose strike price is the same as the current market price

Examples of At the Money Options

  • A 550 call when the current stock price is ~550
  • A 20 put when the current stock price is approximately 20
  • In selling the option you will essentially break even

In the money options are options whose strike price is such that the option is has instrinsic value at current market prices

Examples of In the Money Options

  • A 550 call when the current stock price is greater than 550
  • A 20 put when the current stock price is less than 20
  • By selling the option you will profit ~ the difference

Option Valuation

How do you price these things?

A stock option's market price is determined by the sum of two factors: Instrinsic value and Extrinsic value

Instrinsic Value

An option has intrinsic value only when it is In the money, and is equal to the difference between the strike price and the current market price

Examples

  • A 50 Call option for a stock that is currently at $51.50 has an intrinsic value of $1.50 per share
  • A 10 Put option for a stock that is currently $5 has an intrinsic value of $5 per share
  • A 20 Call option for a stock that is currently at $15 has no intrinsic value

Extrinsic Value

An option has extrinsic value when there is uncertainty in the future price of the underlying stock

Factors that affect extrinsic value are:

  • time to expiration
  • volatility of the underlying stock
  • variation in interest rates

Black-Scholes Model

Bringing rigor to option pricing

Until recently, pricing the extrinsic value of an option was more an art than a science

In 1973 Fischer Black and Myron Scholes published their paper, "The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities" in which they derived a partial differential equation to estimate the price of options over time

  • C(S,t) = value of a call option at stock price S and time t
  • S = Price of underlying stock
  • t = time to evalute price (usually the present)
  • N(x) = the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution
  • K = strike price of option
  • σ = underlying volatility of the stock
  • Γ = rate of free return (interest rate)

Additional Options Strategies

Covered Call Strategy

A conservative trading strategy where you believe that the underlying stock will appreciate over time. In addition to buying the stock itself, you write out of the money call options for that stock.

Example

  • RY is currently trading at $60 and you are bullish on the stock long term. You want to own RY to capture the benefits of dividends but would also like to maximise your rate of return if RY rises, and reduce your downside if RY falls slightly.
  • You decide to employ the covered call strategy by buying 100 shares of RY's stock and simultaneously writing an Out of the Money Call Option with a strike price of $70 for $2.50 a share.
  • This nets you a $250 premium upfront

If RY goes down the option expires worthless and you keep the $250 to offset your capital loss on the stock

If RY goes sideways the option expires worthless and you keep the $250 as profit

If RY rises to just under $70 the option expires worthless and you keep the $250 plus your capital gain on the stock

If RY rises above $70 the option is excercised and your 100 shares of RY are sold at $70. You keep the $250 plus the $7000 from selling the shares

If RY looks like it will rise significantly more than $70 you close your option position at a slight loss and roll your call options forward to a higher strike price, getting another premium in the process

The Straddle

Allows you to profit from either high volatility (long straddle) or low volatility (short straddle) no matter which way the stock moves

Example

  • RY is currently trading at $60 and you believe that there will be movement, but you're not sure which way it will go.
  • You decide to employ the long straddle buying both a call option and a put option at a strike price of $60 for a cost of $2.50 per share or $250 overall.

If RY goes down to $50 then you ignore the call and sell the put for $10 a share, netting $750 profit

If RY goes sideways both options expires worthless and you lost $250

If RY rises to $70 then you ignore the put and sell the call for $10 a share, netting $750 profit

A short straddle is similiar except you write a call and a put at $60, netting a premium of $250. If the stock doesn't move you keep it.

Of course their are many more strategies that can be tailored to your needs. Remember the more complicated the option setup the more likely you are to make a mistake!
